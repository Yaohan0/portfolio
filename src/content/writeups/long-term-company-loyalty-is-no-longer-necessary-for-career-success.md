INTRODUCTION  



The World Economic Forum (WEF) stated that “The linear ladder – one company, regular promotions, steady climb – is fading.”This suggests that long-term company loyalty is not an essential workplace expectation today. In the past, work success often meant staying with a company for 10 to 20 years to gain promotions and job stability. However, this path is less realistic today, as the report from WEF also finds that 39% of workers’ core skills will change by 2030, showing that adaptability matters more than simply staying with one company. As a polytechnic student preparing to enter the workforce, I find this topic crucial to understanding how to stay relevant and competitive in the workforce. I believe long-term loyalty to a company is a workplace expectation that is outdated, as workers can build transferable skills, seek fair rewards, and explore non-linear paths. 



 



DISCUSSION 

 



Job Mobility Builds Better Skills  



Firstly, the expectation of staying loyal to one company is less necessary as job mobility helps workers develops broader skills. In the past, staying loyal to a company limited employees to mastering one skillset. Nowadays, with advanced technology and digital tools, workplaces are rapidly evolving. When working in different companies, employees gain more experience and skills through the exposure of different teams they meet, projects they do, and different problem-solving methods.  



 MOM’s Job Vacancies 2025 report found that academic qualifications were not the main determinant for 79.6% of vacancies, Showing that employers value skills and experience. This is especially true in the cybersecurity field, where someone who stays in one company may only learn to secure that organisation’s systems and network environment, whereas someone who works or interns in multiple companies is exposed to different security risks, tools, and policies. These varied experiences can make workers more attractive to employers. 



 



Loyalty Does Not Guarantee Rewards  



Secondly, long-term loyalty to a company does not always guarantee benefits. In the past, loyalty was often linked to promotions and job security. However, workers today may still face limited salary growth and heavier workloads. An article published by Duke Today highlighted how managers were more likely to assign unpaid work to loyal employees because they were seen as more likely to accept it. This shows that loyalty can create higher expectations instead of fair rewards. With this knowledge, employees may not view loyalty as a safe career strategy. Based on my observations, even professionals like lecturers may leave organisations for better career opportunities, salary progression, or healthier work environments. This shows that employees may choose workplaces that recognise their value, rather than staying loyal without guaranteed advancement. Hence, long-term loyalty is no longer a workplace expectation that is essential today. 



 





Support for Non-Linear Careers  



Lastly, long-term company loyalty is less necessary as society today supports non-linear careers. Digital platforms, freelancing, and entrepreneurship help to build income and personal brands outside of traditional employment, no longer needing to follow the fixed path of climbing up the corporate ladder. 



For example, MySkillsFuture supports Singaporeans in continuous learning, career switching, and skills upgrading throughout their lives. This shows that workers are encouraged to keep adapting instead of staying permanently in one company. Content creators such as DRZY also show that individuals today can build personal brands and income via social media posts and livestreams. I have observed on social media of young adults pursuing freelancing, content creation, or online work alongside their studies. This makes long-term company loyalty less necessary as a workplace expectation as career success can come from multiple pathways, not just one employer. 



 





CONCLUSION 



In conclusion, long-term company loyalty is no longer a workplace expectation that is necessary. Job mobility builds adaptable skills, while loyalty does not always guarantee fair rewards, and society today supports more flexible career paths. This does not mean loyalty is worthless, but employees should not depend on it, but rather develop relevant skills, seek fair opportunities, and remain open to different pathways.
